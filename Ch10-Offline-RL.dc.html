Chapter 10 · Offline RL & Imitation

Learning when you can't touch the world

Hospitals won't let an ε-greedy policy dose patients; factories won't let one drive robots into walls. Often all you get is a logged dataset — and the moment the environment goes away, a quiet assumption underneath everything since Chapter 3 breaks. This chapter is about what breaks, why copying the data is the honest baseline, and how offline RL cautiously does better.

Part 1

Behavior cloning — and why it drifts

The simplest use of a demonstration dataset ignores rewards entirely: treat it as supervised learning, . This is behavior cloning, and when data is plentiful and expert, it is embarrassingly effective — it is also, not coincidentally, exactly what SFT does to a language model. Its failure mode is structural: the clone is trained on the expert's states, but at test time it visits its own. One small mistake leads somewhere the data never covered, where the next mistake is bigger — compounding error. Errors grow roughly quadratically in horizon, versus linear for ordinary supervised learning.

Analogy

Learning to drive by watching a parent who never leaves the lane. The first time you drift six inches, you're in a state you've never seen handled — and your correction is a guess that drifts you further. The expert's competence hid exactly the data you needed most.

In the wild

DAgger's fix — roll out the learner, ask the expert to label the states it actually reaches — is standard in robot imitation. And every LLM lab relearned the lesson: SFT-only models drift in long generations; post-training (Chapter 11) exists partly to patch cloning's compounding errors.

Part 2

Why off-policy ≠ offline

Q-learning is off-policy — so can't we just run DQN on the logged data? No, and the reason is precise. The Bellman target contains : a max over all actions, including ones the dataset never shows. On those, Q is unconstrained fantasy; the max seeks out its most inflated fantasy; and — here is the killer — online RL would self-correct (the agent tries the action, reality disappoints, the value comes down), but offline there is no reality left to disappoint. Errors don't just persist, they propagate through bootstrapping:

This is the same maximization-of-noise disease from Chapters 4 and 7, with the immune system removed. Every serious offline algorithm is some form of pessimism toward the unknown — the mirror image of exploration's optimism, and correctly so: online, trying the uncertain thing teaches you; offline, trusting the uncertain thing can only mislead you.

The arc of the course, in one line

Online exploration: optimism under uncertainty (Chapter 9). Offline learning: pessimism under uncertainty (this chapter). Same quantity — uncertainty — opposite signs, depending on whether reality gets a vote.

Part 3

Three shapes of caution

CQL — push the fantasy down. Add a term that lowers Q on actions the policy likes but the data lacks, and raises it on actions in the dataset: . The learned Q becomes a conservative lower bound — out-of-distribution actions stop looking like free money.

IQL — never ask about unseen actions. Replace the max with expectile regression on values of actions in the data: an upper-expectile V(s) behaves like "the best the dataset does from here," no OOD query ever issued. Then extract the policy by advantage-weighted cloning — copy the data, but copy the good parts harder.

Decision Transformer — stop bootstrapping entirely. Treat trajectories as sequences with return-to-go tokens, and train a transformer to predict actions. At test time, prompt it with a high desired return and let it act the part. RL as conditional sequence modeling — no Q, no Bellman, no pessimism term, just a language model of behavior.

# Decision Transformer, the whole idea in one line of prompting
action = model(returns_to_go=900, states=history_s, actions=history_a)  # "play like a 900-return agent"
Analogy

Three chefs with only a recipe archive: one marks unlisted ingredient combos as risky (CQL), one only ever remixes recipes on file, favoring the five-star ones (IQL), one reads the archive as literature and writes in the style of the best reviews (DT). None may taste anything.

In the wild

Sepsis treatment policies from ICU records, recommender updates from interaction logs, robot skills from teleoperation archives — and DPO (next chapter) is offline RL's philosophy applied to LLM alignment: extract a better policy from fixed preference data, no environment in sight.
