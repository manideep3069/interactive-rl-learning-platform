Chapter 11 · RL for Language Models

The chapter the field moved into

Recast a language model as a policy — states are token prefixes, actions are next tokens, an answer is an episode — and everything you've learned snaps into place. This chapter follows the modern pipeline: RLHF turns human preference into reward and optimizes it with Chapter 6's PPO; DPO shortcuts the whole apparatus; GRPO streamlines it for reasoning; RLVR swaps learned reward for ground truth. This is where RL and the frontier of AI are currently the same subject.

Part 1

RLHF — reward from preference

"Helpful and honest" has no reward function, but humans can compare two answers. The Bradley–Terry model turns comparisons into a trainable scalar reward : the probability that answer A beats answer B is , so fitting the comparisons fits the reward. The classic three-step pipeline:

The KL term matters because is a learned reward — a lossy proxy. Push hard enough and the policy finds text the reward model loves and humans don't: sycophancy, padding, confident nonsense. That is reward hacking (Goodhart's law with gradients), and the β-weighted leash is the first defense. You know the optimizer already — the clip from Chapter 6 is what keeps each step small; the KL is what keeps the destination close.

Analogy

A student graded by a teaching assistant who only skims. Optimize gently and you write clearer essays; optimize ruthlessly and you learn what the TA skims for — bold topic sentences, confident tone — and the essays get worse while the grades get better.

In the wild

InstructGPT (2022) proved a 1.3B RLHF'd model could be preferred over a 175B raw one — alignment as capability. Every major assistant since has shipped through some version of this pipeline. PPO earned its "most-run algorithm in production" title here.

Part 3

GRPO and RLVR — the reasoning recipe

For math and code, you don't need a learned reward at all — run the unit tests, check the boxed answer. That is RLVR: reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, the idea behind DeepSeek-R1 and the reasoning-model wave. And with rewards this clean, PPO's critic — a second full-size network estimating baselines — starts to look like overhead. GRPO deletes it: sample a group of G answers per prompt, and let the group grade itself:

Each answer's advantage is how it did relative to its siblings — Chapter 5's baseline, computed from the batch instead of learned by a critic. Everything else is PPO's clipped surrogate. Half the memory, no critic to train, and a curriculum for free: prompts the model always solves (or never solves) yield zero advantage spread and teach nothing; learning concentrates exactly at the frontier of ability.

# GRPO on one prompt, in caricature
answers = [model.sample(prompt) for _ in range(8)]      # the group
rewards = [1.0 if verify(a) else 0.0 for a in answers]  # RLVR: run the checker
adv = (rewards - rewards.mean()) / (rewards.std() + 1e-4)
loss = ppo_clip_loss(model, answers, adv)               # Chapter 6, unchanged
The open problems, honestly

Verifiers get gamed (formatting tricks, test-case memorization); entropy collapses as the policy sharpens onto known solution paths — pass@1 rises while pass@128 stalls, the exploration debt of Chapter 9 coming due; and outcome-only reward gives one bit for a thousand-token derivation — the credit-assignment problem at its cruelest. The GRPO successors streaming past on the radar — DAPO, GSPO, and friends — are largely patches to these three wounds.

Analogy

Grading on a curve, per problem: eight students attempt it, and each is scored against the group's spread. Problems everyone aces — or everyone flunks — grade to zero and teach nothing. All signal concentrates where the class is split.

In the wild — 2025–26

DeepSeek-R1 showed pure RLVR could elicit reasoning — self-checking, backtracking, longer chains — without being taught them. GRPO-family training now sits behind essentially every frontier reasoning model, and "what's verifiable?" has become the question that decides what AI gets better at next.
