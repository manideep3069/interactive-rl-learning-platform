Where the game is defined

Everything in RL — from a Q-table to PPO — is an answer to one question posed in one language. This chapter teaches the language: agents and environments, Markov decision processes, returns, value functions, and the Bellman equations that hold the whole field together.

Part 1

The loop

An agent lives inside an environment. At each time step it observes a state , picks an action , and the environment answers with a reward and a next state . That's it. That loop, repeated, is the entire stage on which reinforcement learning happens.

Environment

The book's running example is FrozenLake: a 4×4 sheet of ice, some tiles are holes, one tile is a gift. States are tile indices (0–15), actions are the four directions, and the reward is 1 for reaching the gift, 0 otherwise. Small enough to hold in your head — rich enough to break your intuitions.

Intuition

RL differs from supervised learning in two brutal ways: nobody tells the agent the right action (only a scalar reward, often delayed), and the agent's own actions decide which data it will ever see. Learning and data collection are the same act.

Analogy

Training a puppy. There is no manual of correct actions — just treats (reward) after good behavior. And crucially, the puppy's own choices decide what situations it ever finds itself in: a dog that never leaves the couch never learns the park.

In the wild — RLHF for chatbots

The loop behind ChatGPT-style fine-tuning is exactly this diagram: the model (agent) writes a response (action) to a prompt (state), a reward model scores it, and the loop repeats millions of times. Same loop, different costume.

Part 2

Markov decision processes

Definition — finite MDP

A finite MDP is a tuple : a finite state set, a finite action set, a discount factor , and a dynamics function

The one load-bearing assumption is the Markov property: the distribution above conditions only on the current , not on the history. The present state is a sufficient statistic of the past — whatever happened before is either baked into or irrelevant. Every algorithm in this book leans on this: it is what lets a value be attached to a state rather than to an entire life story.

Tasks come in two flavors. Episodic tasks end — FrozenLake ends when you drown or deliver. Continuing tasks run forever. The discount factor lets us treat both with one formalism, as you're about to see.

Analogy

A chess position. To pick your next move you never need to know how the pieces got there — the board in front of you is a complete summary. That is the Markov property: the present state carries everything relevant about the past.

In the wild — AlphaZero

AlphaZero treats Go, chess and shogi as MDPs: the board is the state, legal moves are actions, and the only reward is +1/−1 at the end of the game. The entire superhuman system rests on the board being Markov.

Intuition

Value functions are the field's great trick: they convert a long-horizon problem ("what will happen over the next thousand steps?") into a lookup ("how good is here?"). Half of this book — Chapters 2, 3 and 4 — is about estimating them. The other half uses them to steer.

Analogy

A strong chess player glances at a position and feels "White is winning" without calculating to checkmate. That instinct is a value function. Their style of play — aggressive, positional — is the policy.

In the wild — AlphaZero's two heads

AlphaZero's network has literally these two outputs: a policy head (a distribution over moves, π) and a value head (who wins from here, v). Chapter 1's two definitions became two tensors.

Analogy

In the wild — navigation and routing

Route planners, network packet routing and airline crew scheduling all solve Bellman equations at scale — shortest-path algorithms like Dijkstra's are value computation on a graph where reward is negative distance.

Part 7 · from the repo

Meet the code

Everything above already lives in algos/value_based/tabular.py. When states and actions are finite, an action-value function is literally a table — one number per pair:

n_states, n_actions = env.observation_space.n, env.action_space.n
Q = np.zeros((n_states, n_actions), dtype=np.float64)   # our estimate of q*(s, a)

And "act greedily, but explore sometimes" — the ε-greedy policy you'll meet properly in Chapter 3 — is six lines:

def epsilon_greedy(Q, s, eps, n_actions, rng) -> int:
    if rng.random() < eps:
        return int(rng.integers(n_actions))   # explore: uniform random action
    qs = Q[s]
    best = np.flatnonzero(qs == qs.max())     # tie-break uniformly
    return int(rng.choice(best))              # exploit: argmax_a Q[s, a]

Notice the tie-breaking: at the start Q is all zeros, so every action ties. Choosing uniformly among the maxima keeps early behavior properly random instead of biased toward action 0 — a tiny detail with real consequences.

Analogy

Picking a restaurant. Most nights you go to your proven favorite (exploit), but sometimes you try the new place (explore) — because your "favorite" is only the best of the ones you've tried.

In the wild — recommender systems

News feeds and ad systems run ε-greedy-style exploration in production: mostly serve what's known to perform, occasionally serve something untested — otherwise new content could never be discovered as good.
