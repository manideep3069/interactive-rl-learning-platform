The objective

Let be a trajectory and its discounted return. The policy's quality is the expected return of the trajectories it generates:

Why bother, when Q-learning exists? Because a parameterized policy handles continuous actions (no argmax over infinitely many options), represents stochastic optima naturally, and is sometimes simply an easier function to learn than values. The catch: looks impossible — θ affects which trajectories happen, and the environment's dynamics sit inside that expectation, unknown and non-differentiable.

Analogy

Coaching a dart thrower by adjusting their stance to raise the average score — you tune the behavior itself, not a scoreboard of every possible throw. Wherever average score climbs steepest, nudge the stance that way.

In the wild — RLHF fine-tuning

Fine-tuning language models from human feedback optimizes exactly J(θ): the policy is the model's token distribution, the reward is a learned preference score, and gradient ascent on expected reward is the training loop behind ChatGPT-style assistants.

In the wild — beyond RL

The same identity drives variational inference, financial risk gradients, and neural architecture search — Google's early NAS literally trained its architecture-proposing controller with REINFORCE. One line of calculus, four fields.

Part 3 · spg.py

Simple Policy Gradient

Estimate the expectation with N sampled trajectories, and hand the gradient to autodiff by building a scalar whose gradient is the estimator — that's all the famous "loss" is. From spg.py:

# g_hat = (1/N) * sum_i  G_i * sum_t  grad log pi(a_t^i | s_t^i)
# every timestep of trajectory i is weighted by the SAME scalar G_i
batch_G = torch.cat([
    torch.full((t["length"],), t["G"], dtype=torch.float32)
    for t in trajectories
]).to(device)

log_probs = agent.log_prob(batch_obs, batch_actions)
loss = -(batch_G * log_probs).sum() / args.num_trajectories   # minimize -J
optimizer.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); optimizer.step()

Two honest warnings about this "loss": its value means nothing (it can go up while the policy improves), and after one optimizer step the data is dead — the estimator was only valid at the θ that generated it. And SPG has a deeper flaw: every action in an episode gets the same credit , including the brilliant move in a game you lost. The variance is enormous.

Analogy

After a tournament, praise every move in the games you won and scold every move in the games you lost — including the brilliant moves in losses. Crude and noisy, but averaged over enough games, directionally right.

In the wild — the honest baseline

SPG is the control experiment of the field: when a new policy-gradient paper claims progress, this algorithm is what sits in the ablation table as the floor. Every improvement in this chapter is measured against it.

Part 4 · reinforce.py

Reward-to-go: don't credit the past

An action at time cannot influence rewards earned before — yet SPG's weight includes them. Causality lets you delete those terms from the estimator (their expectation is provably zero), keeping only each action's own future:

Same expectation, strictly less noise. In code, the entire difference between the two files is what multiplies log_probs:

spg.py — one scalar per trajectory
torch.full((t["length"],), t["G"])
# every step weighted by G(tau)
reinforce.py — one weight per timestep
t["G_t"]   # reward_to_go(rewards, gamma)
# each step weighted by ITS future

Run both on CartPole with the same seeds and watch REINFORCE pull ahead — that's Project 3.

Analogy

Don't blame this morning's breakfast for yesterday's exam grade. An action can only be credited with what happened after it — rewards already banked before the action are pure noise in its gradient.

In the wild — every implementation

Reward-to-go is universal: SpinningUp, CleanRL, Stable-Baselines3 all compute it in the same backwards loop you see below. It is the first variance reduction and it is free — strictly less noise, zero bias.

Part 5 · vpg.py

Baselines and the advantage

Second variance killer: judge actions relative to expectations. Subtract any state-dependent baseline from the weight — the gradient's expectation is untouched:

Deep dive — why baselines are free

Probabilities sum to one, and the gradient of a constant is zero. So the baseline adds zero bias — but chosen well (), it centers the weights and slashes their variance. The crucial restriction: b may depend on the state, never on the action — an action-dependent baseline would not factor out of the sum.

The best-known choice is the state value itself, giving the advantage — "how much better than my usual was that?":

Of course is unknown — so vpg.py learns a second network, the critic , by plain regression onto observed returns. Actor and critic, the pattern PPO inherits whole:

adv = traj["G_t"] - V_traj            # A_t = G_t - V_phi(s_t)   (baseline: no grad)

# actor:  push up log-probs of above-average actions, push down the rest
actor_loss = -(advantages * log_probs).sum() / args.num_trajectories

# critic: regression toward the observed returns
critic_loss = 0.5 * ((value_targets - V_pred) ** 2).sum() / args.num_trajectories
Intuition

With raw returns, an agent in a generous world reinforces everything it did — good and bad alike — because all returns are positive. The advantage recenters the verdict: above expectation, push up; below, push down. Same expectation, radically better signal-to-noise.

Analogy

Grading on a curve. "Scored 80" means nothing until you know the class average from that position; the advantage asks "was this action better than expected from here?" — and pays gradient only for the surprise.

In the wild — the critic everywhere

The learned baseline V(s) is the "critic" in every actor-critic system: AlphaGo's value network baselined its policy updates, and the value head in RLHF's PPO plays exactly this role. Chapter 6 makes it one of three terms in a single loss.

Part 6

GAE: the λ dial returns

is the Monte Carlo advantage: unbiased, noisy. The TD error is the one-step advantage: biased by the critic's errors, but quiet. Generalized Advantage Estimation blends the whole spectrum with a decay λ — eligibility traces reborn:

Computed in one backward pass, exactly like reward-to-go — from vpg.py:

def compute_gae(rewards, values, gamma, lam):
    T = len(rewards)
    advantages = np.zeros(T, dtype=np.float32)
    last_gae = 0.0
    for t in reversed(range(T)):
        next_value = values[t + 1] if t + 1 < T else 0.0   # V(terminal) = 0
        delta = rewards[t] + gamma * next_value - values[t]
        advantages[t] = last_gae = delta + gamma * lam * last_gae
    return advantages

The file also ships two "very common" extras worth knowing by name: advantage normalization (mean 0, std 1 per batch — keeps gradient scale steady) and multiple critic epochs per iteration. With actor, critic, and GAE assembled, you are one idea away from PPO.

Analogy

Blending eyewitness accounts of different lengths: short accounts are reliable but see little; long accounts see everything but embellish. λ mixes all horizons with geometric weights instead of trusting any single witness.

In the wild — GAE as the default

Schulman's GAE (2016) is the advantage estimator in essentially every PPO deployment — OpenAI Five, robotic locomotion, RLHF. The λ dial from Chapter 3's traces returns wearing a policy-gradient coat.
