Chapter 3 · Tabular Learning

Learning from experience

No more model. From here on the agent knows only what happens to it. This chapter builds the classics — Monte Carlo, TD, SARSA, Q-learning, and their n-step and eligibility-trace generalizations — every one of them implemented in the repo's tabular.py. And halfway down, you get to watch one learn.

Part 1

Monte Carlo: just average what you saw

A value is an expected return — so estimate it the honest way: run whole episodes, compute actual returns, and average. With a constant step size , "average" becomes an incremental nudge toward each new sample:

In tabular.py, the returns are built backward from the end of the episode with the Chapter 1 recursion — one pass, no re-summing:

# after the episode ends: walk it backward, G eats its own tail
G = 0.0
for t in reversed(range(len(states))):
    G = rewards[t] + args.gamma * G                    # G_t = R_{t+1} + gamma * G_{t+1}
    Q[states[t], actions[t]] += args.learning_rate * (G - Q[states[t], actions[t]])

One catch: to keep learning about all actions, the policy must never stop trying them. That's the ε-greedy policy from Chapter 1 — greedy with probability , uniform otherwise — and it's the exploration story for this whole chapter. MC control is GPI with sampled evaluation: play an episode under the ε-greedy policy, update Q toward the observed returns, repeat.

The costs

MC targets are unbiased — they are the definition of the value — but high-variance (a whole episode of randomness in every sample), and you must wait for the episode to end before learning anything. Both problems have the same cure, and it's the big idea of this chapter.

Analogy

Judging a restaurant only after the whole meal, including dessert. No opinion until the episode ends — then the complete bill and experience update your rating. Honest, but you learn nothing mid-meal.

In the wild — poker and game rollouts

Monte Carlo rollouts power modern poker bots and game engines: play thousands of complete hands from a spot, average the outcomes, and the average is the value estimate. Unbiased, embarrassingly parallel — and episode-complete by construction.

Part 2

Temporal difference: learn a guess from a guess

The recursion says the tail of a return is itself a return — so instead of waiting to observe , substitute your current estimate of it. This is bootstrapping, and it turns the update into something you can apply after a single step:

The TD target is biased (your estimate of is wrong, especially early) but has far lower variance — only one step of randomness instead of a whole episode. It's the opposite trade from MC, it learns online, and empirically it usually wins. DP bootstrapped from a model; TD bootstraps from experience.

Analogy

Revising your ETA the moment you hit traffic. You don't wait to arrive to know you'll be late — you update your estimate from the next estimate down the road. A guess corrected by a slightly-better guess.

In the wild — TD-Gammon and dopamine

Tesauro's TD-Gammon (1992) reached top-human backgammon learning purely from TD errors — the result that convinced a generation. Then neuroscience found dopamine neurons fire in the exact pattern of a TD error: the brain seems to run this algorithm.

Part 3

SARSA and Q-learning

Apply TD to action-values and you get control. Two ways to build the target from the next state — one tiny difference, two different philosophies:

SARSA — on-policy

where is the action the ε-greedy policy actually takes next. Learns the value of the behavior it really follows — exploration mistakes included.

Q-learning — off-policy

bootstraps from the best next action, whatever the agent actually does. Learns about the greedy policy while behaving ε-greedily — the Bellman optimality equation as an update rule.

Here is Q-learning in the repo, whole. Read it slowly — this is the most important dozen lines in classical RL:

def run_episode_q_learning(env, Q, args, eps, rng):
    s, _ = env.reset()
    while True:
        a = epsilon_greedy(Q, s, eps, env.action_space.n, rng)   # behave: explore
        s_next, r, term, trunc, _ = env.step(a)
        bootstrap = 0.0 if term else float(np.max(Q[s_next]))    # learn: greedy max
        Q[s, a] += args.learning_rate * (r + args.gamma * bootstrap - Q[s, a])
        if term or trunc:
            break
        s = s_next

Two details worth tattooing somewhere: the bootstrap is zeroed at terminal states (there is no future to estimate — forgetting this is the classic RL bug), and behaving (epsilon_greedy) is a different line of code from learning (np.max). That separation is what "off-policy" means.

Intuition — the cliff

Walking beside a cliff, Q-learning learns the shortest path along the edge (the greedy policy never slips — only the ε does), while SARSA learns to keep a safe distance, because its target includes the days it stumbles. Neither is wrong; they answer different questions.

Deep dive — when does this actually converge?

Q-learning converges to with probability 1 under two conditions. (1) Infinite exploration: every state–action pair is visited infinitely often — this is what ε-greedy with buys you. (2) Robbins–Monro step sizes: (steps large enough to travel anywhere) and (steps small enough for the noise to average out). A constant α, as in the repo, violates the second — the estimate never fully settles, jittering forever in a band around . In practice that's a feature: it keeps adapting if the world drifts. For SARSA, add a decaying ε (GLIE: greedy in the limit) and it converges to too.
Analogy

SARSA learns to drive the way you actually drive, wobbles included — so it stays away from cliff edges. Q-learning learns the racing line of a perfect driver, even while driving sloppily — optimal in theory, occasionally in the ravine during practice.

In the wild — safety on real hardware

On physical robots, exploration accidents cost real money, so on-policy caution (SARSA-style) matters. Q-learning's off-policy target is what scaled up into DQN's Atari results (Chapter 4) — where crashing is free.

Part 5

Between MC and TD: n-step and traces

TD looks one step ahead; MC looks all the way. Everything in between exists too — take real rewards, then bootstrap:

n-step SARSA uses this as its target — the repo implements the bookkeeping (a sliding window of the last n transitions) exactly as in Sutton & Barto §7.2. Small n → low variance, more bias; large n → the reverse. The sweet spot is usually in the middle, and this dial reappears as GAE's λ in Chapter 5.

Eligibility traces get the same effect without picking one n — they mix all n-step returns, weighted geometrically by . The mechanism is beautifully local: keep a trace of how recently each pair was visited, and broadcast every TD error to all of them at once:

# SARSA(lambda), the inner loop  (delta = r + gamma * Q[s',a'] - Q[s,a])
if args.trace_type == "accumulating":
    E[s, a] += 1.0        # visits stack up
else:
    E[s, a] = 1.0         # "replacing" trace: pin to 1

Q += args.learning_rate * delta * E    # ONE error updates EVERY recently-visited pair
E *= args.gamma * args.lambda_         # all traces decay together

At the trace dies instantly and you're back to one-step SARSA; at credit flows all the way back and you approach MC. One parameter, the whole spectrum.

Analogy

Assigning blame after a lost game. One-step TD scolds only the final blunder; Monte Carlo scolds every move of the game equally; traces spread blame backwards with fading intensity — recent moves get most of it.

In the wild — modern deep RL defaults

Every serious system picks a point on this dial: DQN variants use n-step returns (Rainbow uses n=3), and PPO's GAE (Chapter 6) is exactly the λ-trace idea reborn for advantages. The bias-variance dial never went away.

Part 6 · from the repo

The training loop, for real

Around every algorithm, tabular.py wraps the same scaffolding you'll see in every serious RL codebase: seeding for reproducibility, a linearly decaying ε, periodic greedy evaluation (report what the learned policy can do, not what the exploring one stumbles into), and logging:

def linear_schedule(start_e, end_e, duration, t):
    slope = (end_e - start_e) / duration
    return max(end_e, start_e + slope * t)   # 1.0 -> 0.05 over half of training

while global_step < args.total_timesteps:
    eps = linear_schedule(args.start_e, args.end_e,
                          args.exploration_fraction * args.total_timesteps, global_step)
    ep_return, ep_len = run_fn(env, Q, args, eps, rng)   # one episode of MC/SARSA/Q-learning
    global_step += ep_len

Explore hard early (ε starts at 1: pure random, like the demo before you press anything), settle down late (ε ends at 0.05: mostly trusting the table). Try it yourself — that's Project 1.

Analogy

A practice schedule with declining supervision: heavy experimentation early (high ε), trusting the learned play late (low ε), with the learning rate cooling in step. The schedule is not decoration — it is the difference between converging and thrashing.

In the wild — every production run

Annealed exploration and learning-rate schedules appear in essentially every published RL result — DQN's ε decays 1.0 → 0.1 over the first million Atari frames. When results don't replicate, the schedule is the first suspect.
