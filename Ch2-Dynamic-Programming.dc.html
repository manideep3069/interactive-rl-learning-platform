Chapter 2 · Dynamic Programming

Planning, when you know the rules

Give me the MDP's dynamics and I don't need to learn anything — I can compute the optimal policy. This chapter is that computation: policy evaluation, policy improvement, and the two great algorithms they combine into. It's also where the proofs live; the ideas here certify everything that comes later.

Part 1

Planning is not learning

Dynamic programming assumes the one thing real agents rarely have: the full dynamics , as a table you can read. Under that assumption the Bellman equations stop being descriptions and become algorithms — apply them repeatedly and they compute value functions for you. No environment interaction, no data, no luck.

The book covers DP briefly; the repo's supplementary/ folder carries the author's fully rigorous treatment. This chapter walks the same road with the key proofs inline.

Analogy

Planning is studying the city map at your kitchen table — you know every street (the model), you just have to compute the route. Learning is being dropped in a foreign city with no map: knowledge only comes from walking.

In the wild — AlphaZero vs robotics

AlphaZero plans: the rules of chess are a perfect model, so its search simulates futures without playing them. A warehouse robot has no such model of friction, wear and clutter — it must learn from real interaction. Same math, opposite information.

Iterative policy evaluation

Chapter 1 left us with a linear system: the Bellman expectation equation, one equation per state. You could invert a matrix — or you could do something far more RL-flavored: start with any guess and repeatedly apply the Bellman equation as an update:

Gossip converging in a village. Each house updates its story from its neighbors' latest versions; after enough rounds of sweeps, every version agrees and the story stops changing. The fixed point is vπ.

In the wild — the critic in actor-critic

Evaluating a fixed policy is half of every actor-critic system (Chapters 5–6): the value network ("critic") does policy evaluation continuously while the actor behaves. Insurers do the discrete version when pricing a fixed underwriting policy.

Intuition

"If deviating from the plan for one step helps, deviating forever helps at least as much." This theorem is the engine of almost all of RL: it's why acting greedily on learned values (Chapter 3), or nudging a policy toward higher-advantage actions (Chapters 5–6), makes things better and not worse.

Analogy

Reviewing your commute with fresh eyes: at each intersection, ask "given what I now know about travel times, is a different turn better?" If yes anywhere, switch. The theorem guarantees switching never hurts.

In the wild — AlphaZero's improvement loop

AlphaZero's search is policy improvement made concrete: MCTS looks one-step-deeper than the raw network and returns better move probabilities; the network then trains to imitate the search. Improve, evaluate, repeat.

Part 4

Policy iteration

Alternate the two moves and you get the first complete algorithm of the book: evaluate the current policy to get , improve it greedily, repeat.

π₀evaluate → v(π₀)improve → π₁evaluate → v(π₁)… → π*

For a finite MDP this terminates, and at the optimum: there are finitely many deterministic policies, each improvement step is strictly better unless the policy is already greedy with respect to its own values — and a policy that is greedy w.r.t. its own value function satisfies the Bellman optimality equation, so it is optimal. No cycles, no infinite descent: a staircase with a top.

Analogy

A sports team's seasons: play a full season under one game plan (evaluation), then let the coaches revise the plan over the off-season (improvement). Repeat until the plan survives review unchanged — that plan is optimal.

In the wild — self-play generations

AlphaZero's training is generalized policy iteration at scale: generation N's network plays out games (evaluation data), training on them produces generation N+1 (improvement), and the cycle ran for ~700,000 iterations for chess.

Part 5

Value iteration

Full evaluation between improvements is wasteful — why polish to convergence if you'll discard next step? Truncate the evaluation to a single sweep and fold the greedy max straight into the update:

This is the Bellman optimality operator, and the same contraction argument applies (a max of contractions is a contraction): geometrically. Stop when a sweep changes nothing much, then read the optimal policy off greedily. In code, value iteration is almost embarrassingly short:

# value iteration, dense-matrix style (P: [S,A,S'] transitions, R: [S,A] expected rewards)
V = np.zeros(n_states)
while True:
    Q_sa = R + gamma * P @ V          # one-step lookahead for every (s, a)
    V_new = Q_sa.max(axis=1)          # Bellman optimality backup
    if np.max(np.abs(V_new - V)) < 1e-8:
        break
    V = V_new
pi_star = Q_sa.argmax(axis=1)         # greedy policy, read off at the end
Generalized policy iteration

Policy iteration and value iteration are two points on a dial: how much evaluation between improvements. Sutton & Barto call the whole dial GPI — evaluation and improvement chasing each other until they agree, and the only place they can agree is . Keep this picture; every algorithm from here on is GPI with the "evaluate" box replaced by sampling.

Analogy

Water flooding a maze from the exit. Each sweep, the water seeps one step further backwards from the goal; a state's depth is its distance-to-reward. Stop when the water level stops rising — then just swim uphill.

In the wild — warehouse routing

Value iteration solves small exact problems everywhere: robot path planning on grid maps, elevator dispatch policies, and inventory control — anywhere the state space fits in memory and the model is known.
