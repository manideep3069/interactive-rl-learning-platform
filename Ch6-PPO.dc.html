Chapter 6 · Proximal Policy Optimization

Squeeze the data, gently

Chapter 5 ended with a confession: after one gradient step, the batch is garbage. PPO's whole ambition is to take many steps on the same batch without breaking anything — importance sampling to make it legal, clipping to make it safe. It is the default algorithm of modern RL, from game agents to RLHF, and it's all in the repo's ppo.py (lineage: CleanRL).

Part 1

Borrowing another policy's data

You have samples from but want an expectation under . Statistics has sold this correction for a century — reweight each sample by how much more (or less) the new policy likes it. That gives the surrogate objective:

At every ratio is 1 and the surrogate's gradient equals the true policy gradient — so maximizing L locally is maximizing J. In code the ratio is computed in log space (stable, and it gives you a KL estimate for free):

_, newlogprob, entropy, newvalue = agent.get_action_and_value(b_obs[mb_inds], b_actions.long()[mb_inds])
logratio = newlogprob - b_logprobs[mb_inds]   # log pi_theta - log pi_theta_old
ratio = logratio.exp()
The catch

The correction is only trustworthy while stays near — the states in the batch were visited by the old policy, and the reweighting says nothing about states the new policy would visit instead. Wander far and the surrogate flatters you while the real objective collapses. TRPO's answer was a hard KL-divergence constraint (a trust region) enforced with second-order machinery. PPO wanted something you can write in two lines.

Analogy

Using last year's customer survey for this year's redesigned product: reweight each respondent by how much they resemble the new customer base. Right on average — but a few extreme weights can dominate the whole estimate.

In the wild — off-policy evaluation

Ad platforms and recommenders estimate "how would the new ranking policy have performed?" from logs of the old policy via these same importance ratios — evaluating without deploying. The variance blow-up you see here is their daily problem too.

Part 2

The clipped surrogate

Here are the two lines. Cap the ratio's reward at the edge of a small interval, and always take the pessimistic branch:

Good action — A > 0

You'd like to raise its probability. Fine — but once , the clipped term stops growing: no further reward for pushing harder. The incentive to overshoot is surgically removed.

Bad action — A < 0

You'd like to suppress it. Same deal in mirror: below the objective flattens. And thanks to the min, if a previous epoch already moved the ratio too far, the objective can still pull it back — the bound is one-sided in your disfavor, by design.

The repo's version looks upside-down because it minimizes a loss — negate everything and min becomes max:

pg_loss1 = -mb_advantages * ratio
pg_loss2 = -mb_advantages * torch.clamp(ratio, 1 - args.clip_coef, 1 + args.clip_coef)
pg_loss = torch.max(pg_loss1, pg_loss2).mean()   # max of losses = min of objectives

That's the whole trick. With the ratio's incentives fenced in, PPO happily runs 4 epochs of minibatch SGD on every batch — the sample-reuse that vanilla policy gradients could never afford.

Analogy

A driving instructor's speed limiter: improve your lap however you like, but the car physically cannot lurch more than ε from the last safe speed. Progress is welcome; leaps of faith are clipped off.

In the wild — OpenAI Five & RLHF

PPO trained OpenAI Five to beat Dota 2 world champions, and it is the workhorse of RLHF — the clip is what keeps a language model from lurching away from coherent text while chasing reward. Arguably the most-run RL algorithm in production today.

Part 3 · from the repo

Advantages on an unfinished story

One structural change from vpg.py: PPO collects a fixed window — 128 steps × 4 environments — not complete episodes. Rollouts end mid-episode, so the GAE loop must bootstrap the tail from the critic and re-zero itself at episode boundaries. Every index in these lines earns its keep:

with torch.no_grad():
    next_value = agent.get_value(next_obs).reshape(1, -1)   # critic values the cut-off tail
    lastgaelam = 0
    for t in reversed(range(args.num_steps)):
        if t == args.num_steps - 1:
            nextnonterminal = 1.0 - next_done
            nextvalues = next_value                          # bootstrap past the window edge
        else:
            nextnonterminal = 1.0 - dones[t + 1]
            nextvalues = values[t + 1]
        delta = rewards[t] + args.gamma * nextvalues * nextnonterminal - values[t]
        advantages[t] = lastgaelam = delta + args.gamma * args.gae_lambda * nextnonterminal * lastgaelam
    returns = advantages + values                            # critic targets: lambda-returns

nextnonterminal is Chapter 4's (1 - dones) doing double duty: it kills the bootstrap and stops the GAE decay from leaking credit across episode boundaries. The returns = advantages + values line quietly builds the critic's λ-return targets from the same pass.

Analogy

Judging a team mid-season. The season isn't over, but you don't pretend it is: you count the games played, then bootstrap the rest from expectations. Cutting a rollout is not ending an episode — the mask below keeps the two cases straight.

In the wild — continuous control

Walking robots and simulated locomotion tasks never "finish" — every PPO implementation slices experience into fixed windows and bootstraps V(s) at the cut. Getting this mask wrong is the classic silent PPO bug.

Part 4

One loss, three jobs

Unlike vpg.py's separate optimizers, PPO sums everything into a single loss with two knobs:

entropy_loss = entropy.mean()
loss = pg_loss - args.ent_coef * entropy_loss + v_loss * args.vf_coef
#      policy    exploration bonus (0.01)       critic (0.5)

Entropy bonus: penalizes overly confident policies, keeping exploration alive — a soft, differentiable cousin of ε-greedy.

Clipped value loss: the critic gets the same "don't move too fast" treatment as the actor (clip_vloss) — same instinct, applied to values.

Guard rails: advantage normalization per minibatch, gradient-norm clipping at 0.5, learning-rate annealing to zero, and an optional early stop when approx_kl exceeds a threshold.

Reading the dashboard

The file logs the vital signs of a healthy run: clipfrac (what fraction of ratios hit the fence — a steady 0.1–0.2 is normal; ~0 means steps too timid, huge means too bold), approx_kl (how far the policy actually drifted per update), and explained_variance (is the critic earning its keep — near 1 is good, near 0 means your baseline is decoration). Learn to read these three and you can debug most PPO runs. Then go break things on purpose: Project 4.
