The cost of not knowing

Every algorithm so far explored by coin flip — ε-greedy here, entropy bonuses there. This chapter takes exploration seriously: bandits, where the dilemma lives in its purest form; regret, which prices it; UCB and Thompson sampling, which solve it near-optimally; and curiosity, which scales it to worlds where rewards are almost never seen.

Part 1

Bandits — RL with the hard part isolated

A multi-armed bandit is an MDP with one state: k arms, each with an unknown reward distribution, pull one per round. No credit assignment, no dynamics — nothing left but explore-versus-exploit. The scorecard is regret: how much you lost to the best arm you didn't know:

Two facts anchor the whole field. ε-greedy with fixed ε keeps exploring forever at a constant rate, so its regret grows linearly — a permanent tax. And the Lai–Robbins bound says nothing can beat : some logarithmic tuition is unavoidable. The gap between linear and logarithmic is the price of exploring stupidly versus deliberately.

Analogy

New in town, choosing a lunch spot daily. Always-your-favorite means never finding the better place two blocks away; a random spot every day means eating badly forever. Good exploration is neither — it is doubt, budgeted.

In the wild

Bandits quietly run the internet: A/B platforms that shift traffic toward winners mid-test, ad and headline selection, clinical trial designs that treat more patients with the arm that seems to work. Anywhere feedback is instant and choices repeat, it's a bandit.

Part 2

Two ways to be smart about doubt

UCB — optimism in the face of uncertainty. Give every arm the benefit of the doubt, sized to the doubt:

Act greedily on the upper confidence bound. Either the optimism was justified (you found a good arm) or it wasn't (you learned so, and the bonus shrinks). Both outcomes pay. Regret: — matching the lower bound. You met this formula inside MCTS last chapter; now you know its name.

Thompson sampling — act on a sampled belief. Keep a posterior over each arm's mean; each round, sample one plausible world from the posterior and act optimally in it:

# Bernoulli bandit, Beta posterior — Thompson in four lines
theta = [beta.rvs(wins[a] + 1, losses[a] + 1) for a in range(k)]  # sample a world
a = int(np.argmax(theta))                                         # act in it
r = pull(a)
wins[a] += r; losses[a] += 1 - r                                  # update belief

Uncertain arms produce spread-out samples that sometimes win the argmax — exploration emerges from doubt itself, with no bonus term anywhere. Empirically often the best simple bandit algorithm; philosophically the cleanest: explore exactly as much as you believe you might be wrong.

Same instinct, three costumes

Optimistic initialization in tabular Q-learning (Chapter 3), UCB's confidence bonus, MCTS's selection rule — all one idea: uncertainty should raise an option's priority, and attention should lower it. When you see a new exploration paper on the radar, look for this shape first.

Part 3

Curiosity — exploration at deep-RL scale

Confidence intervals need counts, and in a continuous state space every state is visited once or never. The deep-RL answer: intrinsic reward — pay the agent for novelty itself, . The neatest trick for measuring novelty without counts is Random Network Distillation: fix a random network , train a predictor to match it on visited states, and pay . Familiar states are well-predicted → no bonus; novel states aren't → bonus. Prediction error as a proxy for "have I been here?"

RND cracked Montezuma's Revenge — the Atari game where DQN scored zero for years because the first reward is dozens of correct decisions deep. But intrinsic rewards have their own pathologies: the noisy-TV problem (a source of randomness is eternally "novel" — the agent stares at static forever), and detachment (curiosity consumed, the agent forgets the way back to the frontier). Exploration remains genuinely unsolved — which is exactly why it's resurfacing in LLM training, where RLVR's exploit-heavy updates are accused of collapsing the very diversity that finds new solutions.

Analogy

A child in a new house, drawn to whatever they can't yet predict — until the TV static teaches them that some surprises never resolve. Healthy curiosity needs both the pull of the unknown and the wisdom to abandon the unknowable.

In the wild — 2026

Today's frontier restates the dilemma at LLM scale: reasoning models trained with verifiable rewards sharpen known solution paths but may under-sample novel ones — entropy collapse, measured in pass@k. The bandit chapter of RL is having a second youth inside post-training.
